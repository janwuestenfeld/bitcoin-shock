Bitcoin Conditional SHOCK Regime Classifier

Walk-forward cell-conditional bias for BTC vs SPY relative performance

Developed by Dr. Jan Wuestenfeld

The Bitcoin Conditional Shock Regime Classifier maps today's (VIX, active macro-shock indicators, era) into one of 84 historical cells and reports the cell's share-positive outperformance distribution at h = 5 / 20 / 60 / 90 calendar days. It is a probabilistic regime classifier, not a point forecaster. Full technical details in the companion paper & methodology repo.

What this does

  • Classifies today's regime: reads VIX and five top-decile macro shocks (oil, dollar, 10Y rate, banking stress, GPR-threat) under era-conditional walk-forward cutoffs with prior-era q99 tail fallback — the methodologically clean no-look-ahead identification.
  • Looks up the historical cell: matches today's regime against 84 era×VIX×shock-type cells from 2014-2025 and reports the cell's historical share of 60-day BTC outperformance over SPY.
  • Reports horizon distribution: share-positive at h = 5, 20, 60, 90 calendar days, with cell sample size for confidence calibration.

How to read the prediction

  • Share-positive ≥ 60%: bullish cell — majority of historical analogues delivered BTC outperformance.
  • Share-positive ≤ 40%: bearish cell — majority of historical analogues delivered BTC underperformance.
  • Near 50%: coin-flip cell — the framework offers no edge here.
  • n < 10 analogues: low-confidence prediction; the cell uses fallback aggregation.

What this does not do

  • Not a price forecaster: reports directional bias and probability, not target prices.
  • Not a same-day β predictor: β is a 60-day rolling object; "today's shock → tomorrow's β" is not the question this answers.
  • Not a shield against novel regimes: at era boundaries the classifier goes silent (~60-day warmup); during ongoing eras the cutoff is fully stable.

Disclaimer: Not financial advice. Past cell-conditional performance does not guarantee future results; historical regimes may not persist.

Methodology Convergence: Four Cutoff Variants

The shock-classifier converged on the walk-forward cutoff with prior-era q99 tail fallback as the methodologically defensible no-look-ahead identification. The table shows the OOS R² contribution from shock-type-conditional cells (M3 over M2 baseline) under each cutoff. Walk-forward delivers the strongest signal precisely because the shock labels are aligned with the information set contemporaneous investors actually had — future observations cannot influence past investor behavior, so they should not influence the model's shock classifications either.
